# Strategy Catalog

Routing table for Amplify (10x) and Build. Each entry is a pointer, not an explanation. Load what's relevant to the problem at hand.

---

## Enterprise Value

- **Brand-as-Moat (Six Levers):** Category Positioning, Proprietary Methodology, Brand-Driven Inbound, Owned Audience, Brand Identity System, Authority Position. Key insight: EV = Earnings × Multiple — brand strategy affects the multiple, not just earnings.
- **Value Builder / SDE Multiples:** Eight drivers that increase business sale price beyond earnings: recurring revenue, monopoly control, customer satisfaction, hub-spoke growth, financial performance, growth potential, Switzerland structure, clean books. Key insight: most owners optimize earnings; buyers pay multiples for risk reduction.
- **Recurring Revenue Architecture:** MRR/ARR compounds optionality — it lowers discount rate on future earnings. Key insight: same product, subscription model = 3-5x higher multiple than transactional.
- **EBITDA Bridge:** Separates owner-specific costs from true business economics. Key insight: the gap between reported EBITDA and adjusted EBITDA is where acquisition value is made or lost.

## Scaling

- **Revenue Architecture:** Linear (headcount) → Leverage (product) → Exponential (platform). Key insight: revenue model determines multiple range, not just revenue size.
- **Flywheel Design:** Self-reinforcing loops where each cycle makes the next cycle cheaper or faster. Key insight: the moat isn't the product — it's the speed of the flywheel relative to competitors.
- **Unit Economics at Scale:** CAC, LTV, payback period, gross margin per unit. Key insight: businesses that look unprofitable at small scale often have unit economics that look better as scale increases — and vice versa.
- **Crossing the Chasm:** Early adopters and mainstream buyers are different species. Key insight: the chasm is crossed by narrowing the beachhead, not broadening the message.
- **The Constraint (Theory of Constraints):** Every system has exactly one binding constraint at any moment. Key insight: improving anything other than the constraint improves nothing systemically.

## Brand Strategy

- **Category Creation:** Define a new category you can lead rather than competing in someone else's. Key insight: the category leader captures ~76% of category value — category definition is more valuable than product differentiation.
- **Proprietary Methodology:** Name and systematize your approach so competitors can't replicate it without copying you. Key insight: a named methodology is defensible IP without patents.
- **Authority Positioning:** Publishing, speaking, and credentials that position you as the reference standard. Key insight: authority compounds — each asset makes the next one easier to earn.
- **Brand-Driven Inbound:** Build content and community that pulls buyers in without outbound spend. Key insight: inbound CAC approaches zero over time; outbound CAC increases.
- **Owned Audience:** Email list, community, or media property you control regardless of platform. Key insight: platform audiences are rented; owned audiences are assets on the balance sheet.

## Competitive Analysis

- **Seven Powers (Hamilton Helmer):** Scale economies, network effects, counter-positioning, switching costs, branding, cornered resource, process power. Key insight: only these seven create durable differential returns — everything else is temporary advantage.
- **Blue Ocean Strategy:** Create uncontested market space by eliminating, reducing, raising, and creating across factors. Key insight: competing in red oceans is a choice, not a given — the alternative is to define the water.
- **Competitive Moat Inventory:** Identify which of the seven powers you hold, which are buildable, which competitors hold. Key insight: moats degrade unless actively maintained — map them annually.
- **Asymmetric Positioning:** Find the dimension on which you're genuinely superior and make that the only dimension that matters. Key insight: you don't win by being better on all dimensions — you win by controlling which dimension defines the category.

## Negotiation

- **BATNA (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement):** Your walk-away option determines your leverage. Key insight: negotiation power comes from the strength of your alternative, not from tactics.
- **Anchoring:** First number offered shapes the entire negotiation range. Key insight: the anchor doesn't need to be reasonable — it needs to be credible.
- **Value Creation Before Distribution:** Expand the pie before splitting it. Key insight: most negotiations fail to find value-creating trades that would benefit both sides — they go to distribution too early.
- **Loss Aversion Framing:** Losses loom larger than equivalent gains. Key insight: frame your offer as preventing a loss, not enabling a gain, to increase persuasive force.

## Systems & Leverage

- **The Algorithm (Musk):** Delete requirements → delete steps → simplify → accelerate → automate. Key insight: the order is non-negotiable — automating a bad process is the most expensive mistake in operations.
- **Leverage Points (Meadows):** Parameters, buffers, stocks, flows, feedback loops, rules, goals, paradigms, transcending paradigms — ranked from least to most powerful. Key insight: most interventions target parameters (weakest leverage); the highest leverage is changing the goal of the system.
- **Idiot Index:** Ratio of final cost to raw material cost. Key insight: a high ratio reveals where others are capturing value in your chain — and where vertical integration creates the most margin.
- **80/20 (Pareto):** 80% of output comes from 20% of inputs. Key insight: the goal isn't to do less — it's to identify the 20% and make it the only thing that matters.
- **Second-Order Effects:** Every action produces first-order and second-order consequences. Key insight: first-order effects are obvious; competitive advantage comes from acting on second-order effects your competitors haven't seen.

## Decision Quality

- **Pre-Mortem:** Assume failure 18 months out, work backward. Key insight: prospective hindsight (imagining failure has already occurred) increases accuracy of risk identification by ~30%.
- **Inversion:** Instead of asking how to succeed, ask how to guarantee failure — then avoid it. Key insight: most failure modes are knowable in advance; inversion surfaces them.
- **Reversibility Test:** Separate reversible (two-way door) from irreversible (one-way door) decisions. Key insight: one-way door decisions deserve 10x the time; two-way doors should be made fast.
- **Decision Log:** Record the reasons for major decisions at the time of making, not after. Key insight: memory is reconstructive — you will misremember why you decided what you did; logs prevent revisionism.
- **Confidence Calibration:** Track predictions vs outcomes to reveal overconfidence or underconfidence. Key insight: most experts are overconfident on specific predictions and underconfident on ranges.

## Resilience & Risk

- **Antifragility (Taleb):** Some systems gain from disorder; others break. Build the former. Key insight: robustness (surviving shocks) is not the goal — antifragility (growing from them) is.
- **Barbell Strategy:** Combine very safe + very aggressive; avoid the middle. Key insight: moderate risk-taking in the middle of the barbell is where most losses occur.
- **Switzerland Structure:** Reduce single-customer concentration, single-supplier dependency, and geographic lock-in. Key insight: concentration risk discounts valuation by 20-40% in M&A — diversification isn't just resilience, it's economics.
- **Scenario Planning:** Build three futures (base, bear, bull), test the strategy against each. Key insight: the scenario you haven't planned for is the one that breaks you — planning for it at least means you've thought about it.
